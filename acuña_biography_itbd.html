From a Little Girl to an Independent Woman:
My Journey

My Childhood Story

     I was born and raised in the province of Isabela.1 I am the eldest of four siblings.2 As the eldest, I naturally took on a responsible role, often looking after my younger siblings.3 However, I am also a quiet and shy girl.4 My favorite pastime was playing with Barbie dolls and creating different roleplays, imagining various stories and characters.5 Like most kids, I loved running around. 6 As a result, I got a pretty big scar on my right knee. 7 I enjoyed watching shows like Barbie, Winx Club, Mr. Bean, Tom & Jerry, Blue’s Clues, and many more.8 I also had my share of embarrassing moments during childhood.9 One of the most unforgettable ones was when I was still in Kindergarten, I accidentally pooped in my underwear while at school.10 Another funny story my father always reminded me of was how I used to mispronounce color Blue, saying “boo” instead.11 Also, even when I was already in Grade 1, I still drank bottled milk and even brought it to school.12 My lola used to have a tindahan/carinderia business so I would often visit because I get to have food and snacks for free.13 One of the most significant moments of my childhood was getting my period at the young age of 9.14 I had no idea what was happening or what to do.15 Thankfully, my mother was there to guide and explain everything to me.16 Life was peaceful, simple and full of warmth back then.17 At the age of 11 and in Grade 5, my family and I moved to Qatar, a completely different place from what I was used to.18 Adjusting to a new country, culture, and school system was very challenging at first.19 I missed my relatives back home, but I eventually learned to adapt and find comfort in my new environment.20

My Teenage Years

     My teenage years in Qatar were a time of curiosity, self-discovery, and uncertainty.1 I start to ask questions such as about who I really am, what I want to become, and what is the purpose of life.2 It’s also a time where I experience significant life events.3 Like most teenagers, I did face pressures – peer pressure and academic expectations.4 Despite this, I made sure to spend time with my family and close friends.5 We would often hangout at malls, museums, parks, cafes, and cultural sites.6 There are also beautiful beaches and desert adventures.7 My family and I frequently moved to different apartments.8 If my memory serves me right, we moved about 5 times.9 Living in Qatar exposed me to a diverse mix of cultures and people.10 However, there are also many Filipinos in Qatar, making it easier to find a sense of home even in a foreign land.11 No matter where I went, I would always see a “kababayan”.12 Living in the Middle East introduced me to their cuisine, which I came to love and now can’t live without it.13 I also experienced many firsts, such as seeing a camel up close and going on a sand dune adventure.14 As high school came to an end, I had to go back to the Philippines to study for college.15 This was both an exciting and nerve-wracking change, as I had lived in Qatar for almost 12 years and had grown attached to my life there.16 Leaving behind the friends and environment I had known for so long was difficult, but I knew that returning to my home country was necessary for my future.17 I left Qatar with my younger sister, and we both began a new chapter of our lives here in the Philippines.18 Since our parents, together with our two siblings, remained abroad, we had to learn how to be independent and responsible for ourselves.19 Despite the challenges of adjusting to a new environment again, I was determined to embrace it.20

My College Experience

     After almost 12 years in Qatar, I went back here in the Philippines to pursue higher education at University of the East – Manila.1 I am currently a 3rd year (irregular) student taking up Bachelor of Science in Information Technology.2 My decision to choose this course was influenced by my interest in computers and technology, as well as promising career opportunities in the IT field.3 Adjusting to college life in Manila was not easy at first as I struggled with homesickness.4 Now, I can say that I have adapted to the fast-paced city life.5 The BSIT program in UE Manila introduced me to various fields, from programming and networking to database management and software development.6 Some subjects were easy to understand because of my prior knowledge, while others pushed me to expand my skills and problem-solving abilities.7 Coding, debugging errors, assignment/projet deadlines, and late-night study sessions have been part of my daily routine.8 Being an irregular student was challenging – I often felt out of place, moving between different classmates and schedules 9 It was not easy, but I am thankful for the friends I met along the way.10 One thing that made my college life easier is living in a dorm, which saves me from the stress of daily commuting.11 While many students enjoyed going to parties and drinking alcohol, I was never that type of student.12 I have also started to collect random trinkets or “anik-anik”, which I once thought was a childish hobby but now see as something sentimental.13 Whenever there are no classes or during vacation, I always go back to our province in Isabela to be with our grandparents.14 Being in the province brings me a sense of peace and nostalgia.15 I also take the opportunity to go back to Qatar to visit my parents and other siblings and reconnect with my life there.16 Looking back, I can see how much I have changed – from the little girl who loved to play with Barbie dolls to a college student learning to be independent.17 The journey has not been easy, but every challenge and experience has shaped me into the person I am today.18 Though I still have a long way to go, I am grateful for everything that has brought me to this point.19 I look forward to building a future in the IT field, equipped with the knowledge and experiences I have gained.20
